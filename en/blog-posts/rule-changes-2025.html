  • Mandatory Driver Cooling Systems

    • After drivers faced extreme heat exhaustion in races like Qatar 2023, F1 is mandating built-in cooling systems in all cars. This should improve driver endurance, but will it add extra weight or complexity to car designs?

  • Limits on Race Drivers Testing Old Cars

    • Teams can no longer excessively test with their race drivers in previous-generation cars. This rule levels the playing field, but it could also impact how well drivers prepare for new regulations.

  • More Running for Rookies

    • F1 is making it mandatory for teams to give more track time to rookie drivers (those with two or fewer race starts). This will help develop young talent, but will teams be willing to take risks on new drivers in crucial practice sessions?

  • Tighter Restrictions on DRS Designs

    • The FIA is cracking down on loopholes in Drag Reduction System (DRS) designs. Expect more uniformity in overtaking mechanics, but could this limit teams' innovation in finding aerodynamic advantages?

  • Tweaks to Qualifying Classifications

    • Minor changes are coming to how qualifying results are determined, especially in cases involving track limit violations and penalties. This should reduce confusion, but will it impact race weekend excitement?

  • Mule Car Tyre Testing

    • With the 2026 regulations introducing a new generation of cars, teams must develop 'mule cars' to test the next wave of Pirelli tyres. This extra testing phase could give teams an edge heading into 2026.

 

What This Means for 2025?

These rule tweaks may not redefine the sport, but they will certainly influence how teams and drivers approach the season. With no fastest lap point, we might see more aggressive last-lap battles. Rookie drivers could get a real chance to shine. And DRS changes might reshape overtaking strategies.
